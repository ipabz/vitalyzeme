  • The Stress Response
  • When you perceive a stress in your life or environment, your body releases short term and longer term stress hormones
  • Short Term: Epinephrine and Norepinephrine
  • Longer Term: Cortisol
  • Stress can be helpful, when you are facing short term stressors in the environment
  • When Stress is maintained over the long term, there are many negative physical and health effects
  • Short Term Stress

  • The 5 B's of MINDSET Stress Management
  •  B1
  • Body
  • Exercise
  • When you feel stress, get physical.
  • More physically fit people tend to deal with stress better, which can help them live longer lives.
  • The more intense the exercise the more therapeutic it is and the faster it will eradicate your stress.
  • Any form of exercise acts as a stress reliever.
  • Benefits of Exercise when stressed
  • Associated with decreased mortality and morbidity
  • Associated with a decrease in anxiety and depression
  • Improves cognitive functioning
  • Increases levels of circulating dopamine, betaendorphin, and serotonin
  • Both exercise and relaxation increase levels of CRH and improve mood
  • B2
  • Breathe
  • Control breathing, control your mind
  • Your state of mind affects your body and breathing.
  • Breathing can change your body and your mind.
  • Learn to control your breathing and you learn to control your mind.
  • Instead of immediately acting in emotion and anger, pause for a moment. Take a deep breath.
  • Then take another.
  • And another.
  • B3
  • Brain
  • Rethink stress
  • How you think and how you act can transform how you experience stress.
  • When you choose to view your stress response as helpful you create the biology of courage.
  • Chasing meaning is better for your health than trying to avoid discomfort. Go after what it is that creates meaning in your life.
  • Perceive stress as MANAGEABLE so that you use the stress response only when necessary and for short periods
  • B4
  • Bestow
  • Give, care, connect, help others
  • When you choose to connect, give, or care for others you create resilience.
  • You can treat your stress by spending time helping neighbors, friends, people in your community.
  • When you connect and have contact with others, your brain produces a chemical called oxytocin, which counteracts harmful effects of the hormone cortisol
  • Altruism and Health
  • Schwartz et al, 2003 : 2016 Presbyterian church members in the U.S. Evaluated as to whether altruistic behaviors are correlated with better health. While no association was found for physical health, giving was better correlated with mental health than was being the recipient of help.
  • • Brown et al, 2003: 423 older couples over 5 years. An association was found between giving help and reduced risk of dying by half even after controlling for health, age, gender and other factors. No such association was found between receiving help and reduced mortality.
  • • Oman et al, 1999 : Volunteer seniors for 2 or more organizations had a 63 percent lower mortality risk than did non-volunteers during the 5-year study period.

  • Practice the Relaxation Response
  • Relaxation Response
  • Discovered by Dr Herbert Benson of Harvard Medical School
  • Practicing the Relaxation Response results in a state that decreases metabolism, blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, turns off inflammatory genes, and turns on protective, anti-inflammatory and anti-cancer genes
  • The effects of the relaxation response
  • turns off your stress response and your sympathetic nervous system, decreasing the inappropriate release of stress neurochemicals and hormones which damage the cells of the body
  • Turns on the rational, intelligent parts of your brain, and turns off the emotional, reactive centers of your brain
  • Induces Neurogenesis - makes the layers of your brain grow thicker
  • turns on protective, anti-inflammatory and anti-cancer genes that support improved regulation of cellular processes, optimizing metabolism, strengthening the immune system and assisting DNA processing
  • Some Benefits of the Relaxation Response
  • Reduce physiological symptoms
  • Diminish anxiety and incapacitating fear
  • Reduce pain and the use of pain medications
  • Reduce nausea/vomiting side effects of chemotherapy
  • Increase feelings of well-being, comfort and control
  • Increase procedure safety
  • Promote healing and recovery
  • How to activate the relaxation response
  • The repetition of a word, sound, prayer, thought, phrase or muscular activity
  • The passive return to the repetition when other thoughts intrude
